# Decision: DIY vs. Contractor

> **Lead summary:** DIY solar can save 30–50% of installed cost ($8,000–$20,000 on a typical residential system) but requires electrical and construction skills, willingness to do permit paperwork, and the ability to safely work on a roof. The right choice depends on your skills, risk tolerance, local regulations, and how much your time is worth. Many successful projects use a hybrid approach: DIY most of the work but hire an electrician for the service panel connection.

## What "DIY Solar" Actually Means

"DIY solar" exists on a spectrum:

| Level | What You Do | What You Hire Out |
|-------|------------|-------------------|
| **Full DIY** | Everything: design, permits, roof work, wiring, panel connection | Nothing (or code-required electrical only) |
| **DIY + electrician** | Design, permits, roof work, all wiring up to service panel | Licensed electrician for service panel connection |
| **DIY install, contractor design** | All physical installation work | Design and permit package preparation |
| **Informed owner** | Detailed oversight, quality checks, commissioning | Contractor does all physical work |
| **Full contractor** | Nothing (or just paying invoices) | Everything |

The most common successful DIY path is "DIY + electrician" — you handle the roof work, racking, panel mounting, and wiring through the conduit run, and a licensed electrician does the service panel connection for $500–$1,500.

## The Cost Case for DIY

**Typical 10 kW system (2026 market):**

| Line Item | Contractor | DIY |
|-----------|-----------|-----|
| Panels (10 × 400W) | Markup included | $2,500–$4,000 (wholesale) |
| Inverter | Markup included | $1,500–$3,500 |
| Racking + BOS | Markup included | $1,500–$2,500 |
| Battery (if included) | Markup included | $8,000–$15,000 |
| Labor | $8,000–$15,000 | Your time |
| Permits | $500–$1,500 | $200–$800 (same) |
| **Total** | **$25,000–$45,000** | **$13,000–$25,000** |

**Typical savings: $12,000–$20,000 on a 10 kW system without battery.**

The federal ITC (30%) applies to both, so the after-tax cost differential is also significant.

## The Case for DIY

You're a good candidate for DIY if:
- You have done electrical work before (house wiring, outlets, panel work) or are willing to learn
- You're comfortable with roof work and heights and have appropriate safety equipment
- You have 40–80+ hours of free time for a typical project
- You're detail-oriented and will read NEC 690 and your jurisdiction's requirements
- Your local AHJ permits homeowner solar installation (most do, but verify)
- You're in a state with straightforward solar permitting (not California, which has complex utility processes)

## The Case for a Contractor

Strong reasons to hire a contractor:
- **Roof complexity**: Steep pitch, fragile tile, or complex layout requires professional experience
- **Jurisdiction complexity**: Some areas (California under NEM 3.0, HOA-heavy communities) have complex permitting with nuances that experienced local contractors navigate better
- **Time constraint**: Contractors can often complete an install in 1–3 days; DIY may take several weekends over months
- **Warranty concerns**: Some manufacturer warranties are voided by non-certified installers (verify before committing to DIY)
- **Permit risk**: In some jurisdictions, unpermitted or improperly permitted work creates significant liability at resale
- **No physical ability**: Roof work is physically demanding and genuinely dangerous

## Contractor Vetting

If you choose a contractor:
- Verify NABCEP certification (North American Board of Certified Energy Practitioners) — the gold standard for solar installers
- Check license status with your state licensing board
- Get 3+ quotes — prices vary 30–50% between contractors for the same system
- Ask for references from installs in the last 12 months
- Verify they pull permits (never work with a contractor who says "we don't need a permit for this")
- Check Yelp, Google Reviews, and the NABCEP installer directory
- Avoid door-to-door solar salespeople — they work on commission and tend to oversell
- National installers (Sunrun, Tesla, SunPower) have consistent processes but often higher prices; local installers often offer better service and competitive pricing

## Contractor Contract Essentials

Before signing:
- [ ] Specific equipment specified by model number (not just brand)
- [ ] Production guarantee (kWh/year) or production estimate in writing
- [ ] Permit responsibility defined (contractor pulls and closes permits)
- [ ] Timeline with milestones
- [ ] Payment schedule tied to milestones (not just a lump sum)
- [ ] Workmanship warranty (typically 10 years for roof penetrations)
- [ ] Manufacturer warranty pass-through: you receive the manufacturer warranty directly, not through the installer
- [ ] Process for punch list / post-install issues

**Red flags in contractor contracts:**
- Payment in full before installation
- No specific equipment listed
- "We'll handle the permits" without specifying who is responsible for fees and corrections
- Arbitration clauses that waive your right to class action or small claims

## The DIY Learning Curve

**What you need to learn for DIY:**
1. **NEC 690** — the electrical code for PV systems (available free from NFPA's free reading access program)
2. **Your AHJ's permit requirements** — call them; most are happy to explain
3. **Racking system installation** — watch manufacturer training videos; most racking companies have excellent YouTube content
4. **String sizing math** — covered in `../../product/concepts/system-design-fundamentals.md`
5. **Single-line diagram drawing** — learn to read and draw the required permit diagram

**Estimated learning and project time:**
- Research and design: 20–40 hours
- Permit package prep: 5–15 hours
- Installation (10 kW system, 2-person crew): 2–4 full days
- Commissioning and monitoring setup: 2–4 hours

## Hybrid Approach: DIY + Licensed Electrician

This is the recommended path for most first-time DIYers:

1. You handle everything: design, permit package, equipment procurement, racking, panel mounting, all conduit runs, and wiring up to but not including the service panel
2. A licensed electrician performs: the service panel connection, AC disconnect installation, and final electrical inspection prep
3. Cost: $500–$1,500 for the electrical work vs. $8,000–$15,000 for a full contractor

This approach:
- Keeps most of the savings
- Reduces risk at the most code-sensitive step
- Usually satisfies AHJ requirements for licensed work at the service panel
- Protects your homeowner's insurance (unlicensed electrical work can void claims)

## Equipment Access: DIY vs Contractor

One practical consideration: some equipment is harder to purchase without a contractor account.

- **Enphase**: Available through distribution, but some distributors require commercial accounts. Check Enphase's "find a distributor" tool — many sell to homeowners.
- **SolarEdge**: Same situation; distribution varies by region.
- **Racking**: Iron Ridge, Unirac, and others sell through solar distributors who often welcome homeowner purchases.
- **Panels**: Most solar distributors will sell to homeowners; wholesale pricing often requires a commercial account. Expect to pay 10–20% more than installers.

**Tip**: CED Greentech, Rexel Energy, and Sonepar are large distributors; call your local branch and ask about homeowner purchases.
