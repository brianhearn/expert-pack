> **Lead summary:** Net metering credits your electric bill for excess solar power you send to the grid. Most US states have some form of net metering policy, but rates and rules vary widely — and some states (notably California with NEM 3.0) have significantly reduced export credits, shifting the economics toward battery storage. Confirm your local net metering tariff before finalizing system size.

# What Is Net Metering?

## How Net Metering Works

When your solar panels produce more power than your home uses at that moment, excess electricity flows to the grid and your utility meter spins "backward" (or the digital equivalent — a bidirectional meter records export). Your utility credits this to your bill.

At night or when your production is low, you draw from the grid normally. Net metering means you're effectively using the grid as a "virtual battery" — storing credit when you overproduce, withdrawing it when you underproduce.

## Net Metering Rates — It's Not 1:1 Everywhere

**Full retail net metering** (1:1): You receive a credit at the same rate you pay for power. Every kWh you export earns a credit worth one kWh at retail. Common in most US states.

**Avoided cost net metering**: You receive a credit at the utility's wholesale power cost (~$0.03–0.08/kWh), not retail (~$0.12–0.40/kWh). Much less favorable.

**California NEM 3.0** (2023+): Export rates dropped to ~$0.05/kWh on average, while retail rates can be $0.30–0.50/kWh. This dramatically changed California solar economics and pushed demand toward battery storage. NEM 3.0 systems earn only ~15–20% of what NEM 2.0 systems earned per exported kWh.

## Enrollment Matters

Net metering enrollment is often a separate step from interconnection approval. Your utility will:
1. Approve the interconnection agreement (permission to connect)
2. Install a bidirectional net meter (usually at the same time, but sometimes separately)
3. Place you on the net metering tariff rate schedule

**Confirm that you are enrolled on the net metering tariff** — some homeowners have discovered they were billing on a standard tariff for months after system commissioning because net metering enrollment was missed.

## Annual True-Up vs. Monthly Billing

Many utilities do an **annual true-up** for net metering accounts:
- Excess credits from sunny months (summer) roll forward
- At year end, any remaining credits are settled at a lower rate (or zeroed out)
- Sizing your system to produce 100–110% of annual consumption maximizes use of credits

**Monthly netting**: Some utilities net within each billing period; unused credits expire at month end. This makes sizing more critical — avoid massive overproduction in summer that gets discarded.
