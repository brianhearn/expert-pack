### faq/general.md

- A complete residential solar system in 2026 costs $15,000–$35,000 before incentives; most 6–10 kW systems fall in the $20,000–$30,000 range.
- The federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC) is 30% of total installed system cost, applied as a dollar-for-dollar reduction in federal income taxes owed.
- The 30% ITC applies to equipment, labor, permitting fees, and battery storage (if primarily solar-charged); it does not apply to the cost of a new roof.
- Solar payback period depends on electricity rates, production, and incentives; typical US residential payback is 6–12 years.
- Net metering allows excess solar production to be credited to your electric bill at a utility-set rate; most US states have some form of net metering policy.
- Net metering rates have declined in California (NEM 3.0), Nevada, and other states — future policy changes can affect solar economics.
- Solar panels do not produce power during a grid outage unless the system includes a battery and a grid-forming inverter (e.g., microinverter with islanding or hybrid inverter).
- A standard grid-tied system without battery will shut down during a grid outage per NEC anti-islanding requirements, even if the sun is shining.
- DIY solar saves 30–50% of installed cost compared to a contractor-installed system.
- Solar is generally not cost-effective for roofs with fewer than 5 hours/day average peak sun or with heavy shading that cannot be mitigated.
- Financing options include cash purchase, home equity loan/HELOC, dedicated solar loans, and solar leases/PPAs; ownership (cash or loan) is required to claim the ITC.
- With a solar lease or PPA, the third-party owner claims the tax credit, not the homeowner.

### troubleshooting/common-mistakes/top-diy-mistakes.md

- Wrong string sizing — using nominal voltage rather than temperature-corrected Voc — is the most dangerous DIY design error; it can cause overvoltage trips or inverter damage.
- String sizing must use the lowest recorded winter temperature at the site (ASHRAE 2% design temperature) to calculate worst-case open-circuit voltage.
- The most common installation inspection failure is missing or incorrectly worded NEC labels (panels, disconnects, conduit, source circuits).
- Mixed MC4 connector brands in the same connection is a code violation and a known arc/fire cause; use the same brand throughout.
- Roof penetrations not properly flashed are the most common source of post-installation roof leaks.
- Lag bolts for racking must achieve minimum 2.5" embedment into rafter wood; mounting to sheathing only will fail under wind load.
- Submitting the utility interconnection application at the same time as the building permit (running both in parallel) saves 4–8 weeks of project timeline.
- The utility interconnection process often takes longer than the building permit process and is the critical path item.
- A failed AHJ inspection for layout deviation (even a small field modification) requires permit revision before re-inspection.
- Installing a battery outside its operating temperature range (e.g., hot garage in summer) can void the warranty.
- MC4 connector polarity reversal (swapping positive and negative) at the string level will not immediately damage the system but will prevent the inverter from producing power.
- Not setting up production monitoring alerts is a post-commissioning mistake: a failed microinverter or offline string can go unnoticed for months.
